Propulsion · 1

The rocket equation

A rocket carries its own reaction mass and throws it backwards. Conservation of momentum — the exhaust gains backward momentum, the rocket gains an equal forward momentum — leads directly to Tsiolkovsky's 1903 result.

Δvachievable velocity change — the budget of all spaceflight
v_eeffective exhaust velocity = I_sp·g₀
I_spspecific impulse (seconds)
m₀ / m_fwet (fuelled) and dry (empty) mass

It comes from integrating m·dv = −v_e·dm. Because Δv depends on the logarithm of the mass ratio, gains get punishingly expensive — the tyranny of the rocket equation. To reach low Earth orbit you need ~9.4 km/s.

Propulsion · 3

Staging — beating the logarithm

Because Δv grows only with the log of the mass ratio, a single tank can't reach orbit: most of its dry mass is empty structure you keep dragging along. Staging throws that structure away mid-flight, so each stage starts with a fresh, favourable mass ratio. Total Δv is simply the sum.

Each upper stage is "payload" to the stage below it, so lower stages must lift everything above. The penalty of an extra stage is more dry mass and complexity, so real rockets settle on 2–3 stages. The payload fraction to LEO is typically just 2–4%.

Orbital mechanics · 1

The two-body problem & vis-viva

Once in space, a vehicle coasts under gravity alone. For one body orbiting a much larger one, the path is a conic section and the motion is governed by energy. The vis-viva equation gives the speed anywhere on any orbit.

vspeed at distance r
μGM, gravitational parameter of the central body
asemi-major axis (orbit size)
εspecific orbital energy (per kg)

Energy alone decides the type of orbit: ε<0 is bound (ellipse/circle), ε=0 is the escape parabola, ε>0 is an unbound hyperbola. Set up an orbit and read its full state on the right.

Orbital mechanics · 2

Kepler's three laws

Empirical in 1609–1619, derived from Newton's gravity decades later, they still describe every closed orbit.

1 · Ellipses

Each planet moves on an ellipse with the Sun at one focus — not a circle, not centred on the Sun.

2 · Equal areas

The line from Sun to planet sweeps equal areas in equal times, so a body moves fastest at perihelion, slowest at aphelion. This is conservation of angular momentum.

3 · Harmonic law

The square of the period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis: T² ∝ a³. Bigger orbits are slower, by a precise rule.

Quick reference

Formula sheet & glossary

Formulas

Glossary

μ (mu)
Gravitational parameter G·M of the central body; what actually appears in the orbit equations.
Semi-major axis (a)
Half the long axis of the ellipse; sets orbit size, energy and period.
Eccentricity (e)
How stretched the orbit is: 0 = circle, →1 = very elongated, 1 = escape.
Periapsis / apoapsis
Closest / farthest points (peri- and apo- + the body: perigee, perihelion…).
Inclination (i)
Tilt of the orbit plane relative to a reference plane.
Vis-viva
The speed-distance-size relation: v² = μ(2/r − 1/a).
Hohmann transfer
Minimum-energy two-burn transfer between circular orbits.
Oberth effect
Burns are most energy-efficient at high speed, deep in a gravity well.
